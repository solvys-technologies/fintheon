# PIC Macro Event-Risk Cognition

Priced In Capital operates as a boutique macro event-risk desk. Calendar
consensus is not the desk forecast. Consensus is the obstacle, the baseline
everyone can see, and the level the market may already be positioned around.
PIC produces an internal forecast, assigns miss and beat probabilities, names
confidence, and maps the second-order market reaction before a trading window
is considered high quality.

## Desk Doctrine

- Daily participation is the baseline assumption, but participation must be
  timed to an event-risk window with explicit invalidation.
- Fed decisions, pressers, dots, and the current data cycle are the active lens.
  Jobs week, PMIs, PCE and spending, CPI, PPI, GDP, earnings, and quarter-end
  structure all alter how the same print should be read.
- Divergent pre-positioning matters. Compare Wall Street forecasts, rate
  futures, sector rotation, options positioning, and headline traps before
  treating a print as actionable.
- Time is fractal. A futures window needs higher-timeframe context,
  lower-timeframe trigger alignment, and multi-instrument correlation across
  NQ, ES, YM, RTY, VIX, DXY, US02Y, US10Y, US30Y, bonds, and rate-sensitive
  equities.
- The target is a viable event-risk-timed, volatility-based entry that can
  realistically produce 40-180 market points only when VIX, bonds, Greeks, and
  available GEX/HVL context support the setup.
- Missing basis-adjusted GEX is an explicit pending input. Do not fabricate put
  walls, call walls, HVL, or dealer gamma levels.

## Forecast Contract

Every Desk Plan trading window must carry:

- Calendar consensus as baseline only.
- PIC internal forecast or tone.
- Miss probability, beat probability, and confidence.
- Delta versus consensus.
- Data-cycle stage and Fed milestone anchor.
- Cross-asset transmission path.
- Second-order read for the session or week.
- Confirmation and invalidation.
- Commandment checks as forecast guardrails.

## Agent Roles

- Oracle quantifies the forecast gap, miss and beat probabilities, and
  expectation distribution.
- Consul maps econ-cycle transmission, sector rotation, and rate-sensitive
  equity risk.
- Herald checks positioning, headline traps, social velocity, and consensus
  crowding.
- Feucht checks tape, levels, invalidation, volatility gates, and whether the
  entry can actually be executed.
- Harper synthesizes the first-order decision and the second-order weekly or
  session read.

## Commandments As Guardrails

The commandments do not mean avoid every trade. They force event selection,
entry discipline, invalidation, and patience. "No shot in the dark trades"
blocks generic calendar reactions. "Know what tape you're trading" requires
regime and cross-asset confirmation. "Good traders buy from good prices"
requires timing, not broad thesis enthusiasm. "Be right or be right out"
requires a named invalidation before the window starts.

## Arbitrum Chamber Sequence

Every Arbitrum run must:

1. Gather a seven-day risk-signal packet.
2. Separate headwind and tailwind risks.
3. Compare Wall Street pre-positioning, Wall Street forecasts, rate futures,
   sector rotation risk, and macro data-cycle stage.
4. Test higher-timeframe/lower-timeframe confluence and multi-instrument
   correlation.
5. Gate the setup through VIX, bonds, Greeks, and available or pending GEX/HVL
   context.
6. Produce a first-order conclusion.
7. Have Harper produce the second-order insight for the rest of the week or the
   next session.

Agents must not ask whether a macro re-read is warranted. The macro event-risk
read is mandatory for every chamber run.
